# 1st-Century Contextual Framework

*Studying the Biblical Texts through a thought-for-thought apostolic 30-95 AD lens.*

## BIBLE STUDY PROMPTS: USER MANUAL AND FRAMEWORK GUIDE

Welcome to the apostolic, thought-for-thought Bible study framework. This repository contains a rigorous, 12,000+ word system prompt designed to force Large Language Models (LLMs) like Grok, Gemini, Claude, and ChatGPT to analyze biblical texts strictly through the grammatical-historical lens of the 30-95 AD Apostolic era.

But why is such a massive, restrictive prompt necessary? Why can't we simply ask an AI to "interpret this bible verse based on the 1st century"? This guide explains the core philosophy behind the prompt, why certain words and concepts are excluded, and how to get the most out of your AI-assisted study.

### THE PROBLEM: THE GRAVITY OF DEFAULT TRAINING WEIGHTS

Large Language Models are prediction engines trained on vast oceans of human text. When it comes to biblical interpretation, the overwhelming majority of English texts in an AI's training data—commentaries, sermons, systematic theologies, and academic articles from the last 500+ years—teach post-Enlightenment, exchange-based, or clinical theology.

The AI's neural pathways are fundamentally hardwired with what we call the **"Majority Report."** This includes:

* **Commercial/Transactional Logic:** Treating salvation as a literal financial exchange (e.g., "paying off a debt to a distant God").

* **Post-95 AD Frameworks:** Using 16th-century Reformation concepts or 11th-century Anselmian Satisfaction theories as the default lens for reading a 1st-century text.

* **Clinical/Analytical Language:** Describing spiritual realities using modern biological, clinical, or systematic terms rather than the organic, relational vocabulary used by the Apostles.

Because this post-95 AD view is statistically dominant, it exerts a massive gravitational pull. The AI will default to it every single time unless explicitly and forcefully restrained.

### THE DANGER: THE "ILLUSION OF COMPLIANCE"

One of the greatest challenges in using AI for biblical exegesis is how easily a human user's phrasing can trigger this majority report. If a user asks a question using modern theological shorthand, the AI's default weights will instantly flood the response.

Worse still is the **Illusion of Compliance**.
If you give an AI a simple rule to "sound like the 1st century," it will easily adopt the *vocabulary* of the Apostolic era (using words like "redemption," "propitiation," or "flesh"). However, beneath that approved vocabulary, it will silently smuggle in its default modern logic. It will use 1st-century words to describe a modern commercial exchange. The output will look correct, but the underlying spiritual mechanics will be entirely anachronistic.

### THE CORE PURPOSE: RESTRICTING AI PROBABILITIES TO THE BIBLICAL TEXTS

At its most fundamental level, an LLM generates text based on mathematical probabilities—connecting words and concepts that appear most frequently together in its training data.

**The core purpose of this framework is to artificially restrict that probability space.** By utilizing this prompt, we isolate the AI's predictive capabilities exclusively to the Biblical texts themselves. We build a walled garden that forces the AI to connect concepts using *only* the grammatical realities of Koine Greek, Biblical Hebrew, and Aramaic.

Inside this framework, the AI is explicitly forbidden from drawing mathematical or semantic connections using any theology, creeds, or systematic consensus developed after 95 AD. The text interprets the text, limiting the interpretation entirely to the Apostolic lens and ensuring that the scriptural reality remains untouched by centuries of later human philosophy.

### THE SOLUTION: THE "SEMANTIC FIREWALL"

To stop an LLM from acting like a 21st-century systematic theologian, the rules cannot just be suggestions; they must be absolute constraints. Think of these exclusions not as censorship, but as guardrails on a steep mountain highway. Here is how this firewall protects your study:

**1. The Principle of Unaltered Consistency**
Our founding presupposition is that God's Word does not contradict itself. Therefore, we do not need later theological "shortcuts" to make verses fit together. This framework forces the AI to do the hard work of harmonizing the text as it was originally written, ensuring that every analysis is an inevitable consequence of the text itself, rather than a forced doctrinal opinion.

**2. Counteracting AI "Drift"**
Our framework acts as a deliberate counter-measure to AI drift, forcing the AI off the "broad path" of internet consensus and back onto the "narrow way" of the apostolic text.

**3. Avoiding Historical Shortcuts**
Throughout history, well-meaning scholars have invented complex vocabulary to explain away apparent tensions in Scripture. While popular, these terms often subtly redefine the original Greek (α) and Hebrew (א) texts. By omitting these modern terms, we prevent the AI from importing centuries of external baggage into your study session.

### NAVIGATING NUANCES AND EDGE CASES

As you use this prompt, especially in small groups or Discord study sessions, you might encounter a few edge cases:

* **"This feels restrictive!"** It is completely normal to feel a bit of friction when you first use this framework. However, these exclusions are purely defensive tools. They are not designed to ban ideas, but to protect the 1st-century lens. Think of it like a water filter: it doesn't restrict your water; it simply removes the sediment so you can drink it pure.

* **"Does this framework argue for a specific denomination?"**
  No. This framework never argues for or against any specific modern theological view. It simply enforces the axiom that the text must agree with itself. Users who prefer to view Scripture through the lens of modern denominational frameworks will naturally self-select out of using this prompt.

* **"Will this work on my preferred AI model?"**
  Yes! The framework relies on foundational prompting logic. However, different models handle the mandatory `<Chain_of_Reasoning>` XML tags slightly differently:

  * **Claude (Anthropic):** Claude is explicitly trained to follow XML tags. It will execute the `<Chain_of_Reasoning>` natively and flawlessly.

  * **ChatGPT (OpenAI) & Gemini:** Both easily handle the massive context window and will reliably generate the `<Chain_of_Reasoning>` block to validate their work before giving you the final answer.

  * **Grok / Local / Other Models:** If a specific AI model gets confused by the XML tags (e.g., trying to write code instead of reasoning), simply modify the prompt to ask the AI to "output its Chain of Reasoning as a bulleted list" instead of an XML block.

### BEST PRACTICES FOR USAGE

* **Share Freely:** You can confidently share this prompt in forums, study groups, or with students. Because the framework is strictly governed by Scripture interpreting Scripture, it provides a highly rigorous, objective baseline for group discussion.

* **Reviewing Outputs:** When reviewing the AI's answers, always check them against this core manual. Ask yourself: Did the AI successfully logically "AND" the passages together, or did it try to sneak in a modern theological shortcut?
